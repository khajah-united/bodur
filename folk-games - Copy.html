The Spinning Top Game

The spinning top is a small wooden piece shaped like a cone with two ends: one wide and one pointed. Its size is smaller than a fist. A nail is fixed at the pointed end to act as its axis during spinning.

It has three types:

1. The regular spinning top

2. The Na’our top

3. The Maghol top

The spinning top is always mentioned along with the “Meshbel.” The Meshbel is a string about one meter long, wound around the top starting from the nail up to the top. Its other end is tied to a small piece of wood or metal, held between the ring and little fingers. The player throws the top, which lands on its nail and spins, while the Meshbel remains hanging between the fingers. It is a fun and entertaining game played individually or in organized groups. One of its variations is called “Al-Fallous.” In this version, one player places his top on the ground as a target, and others try to pierce it with the nails of their tops. The piercing is called “Anboob.” If a player misses, he must catch his spinning top using the Meshbel; otherwise, his top is exposed to damage. Some players skillfully make the top spin in their hands. The best of all tops is the “Zakid,” which produces a humming sound while spinning.

Al-Muqsi Game

Al-Muqsi is a fun group game played by boys during the day. It includes:
1. Al-Muqsi: a wooden piece pointed at both ends, about 15 cm long and 3 cm thick.
2. Al-Matta’a: a thick stick about 60 cm long.
3. Al-Kour: a horizontal hole in the ground the length of the Muqsi.
4. Al-Sheesh: a line drawn near the Kour.

Game Explanation:

Players are divided into two teams, and a draw decides who starts. The leader throws the Muqsi away from the Kour, then asks a player to choose a word (“Allah” or “Sayfa”). He flips the Matta’a along the ground, chanting alternately until it touches the Muqsi. If the chosen word matches, that team wins the turn. The winning team then plays by placing the Muqsi in the Kour, hitting it lightly to lift it, then striking it hard to send it flying. The opposing team tries to catch it in their hands or clothes. If caught, they score a point; if not, they throw it back toward the Matta’a. Scoring continues, and every three points are called a “Ruh” (soul). The opposing team must eliminate all souls to win. A variation called “Al-Ma Tou’a” is played without a Kour, hitting the Muqsi directly by hand. Another related game involved spinning tops (Balbool), which boys struck repeatedly to make them hum. These games have now disappeared.

Al-Kafoud

Known among Kuwaitis, Al-Kafoud is a palm frond stick stripped of leaves and thorns. Straight at one end and curved at the other, with a wide tip, it was used as a playful toy for children. Some decorated it with colorful fabric scraps tied along a string stretched between its ends. Others added small sticks called “Sikan” at the top and bottom, connected with decorative threads, creating a colorful shape carried proudly by children as they walked.

Al-Darbaha (Boys’ Game)

Al-Darbaha is a circular frame originally from bicycle wheels. The name comes from the Kuwaiti word “to roll” (tadrabah). Boys used sticks to push and guide the rolling frame, sometimes using strong wires twisted around it for control. They raced their Darbahas over distances, competing to knock over each other’s frames. It gave them the same excitement as owning a car or bicycle today.

Al-Heela Game (for Girls)

This is a game played by two or more girls in multiple stages. A rectangle is drawn on the ground and divided into six squares (houses). The player stands at the first square holding a flat piece of stone or pottery called “Al-Rabaza,” while her opponent waits outside the squares for her turn. The player throws the Rabaza into the first house, then hops into it, kicking the Rabaza to the second house, continuing to hop through the third, fourth, and so on until the last. She must return to her starting point without stepping on the dividing lines, using her second foot, or letting the Rabaza land on or outside the lines. If she fails, her opponent takes the turn. In the next round, she throws the Rabaza into the second house and repeats the process, then the third, fourth, and so on until the sixth. In all rounds, she hops through all squares on one foot. Later, she stands at the first house with her back turned and throws the Rabaza randomly. If it lands in a house, that house becomes hers, and she can step on it with both feet when passing. She continues throwing until she owns all houses where the Rabaza lands. The game then restarts and continues in turns with her friends.
