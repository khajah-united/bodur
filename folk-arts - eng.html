Folk Arts Gathering

Al-Fareesa Dance

“Al-Fareesa” (meaning little mare) is a famous traditional dance performed by a group of men. A doll shaped like a horse is made, covered with colorful fabrics and decorated with jewelry. A dancer wears it around his neck and performs inside it, usually at the center of the group, moving to the rhythms of drums and tambourines. The dance is performed during national and religious celebrations. The horse sways right, left, forward, and backward, as if avoiding attacks. One man with a sword tries to strike the rider, while an old man with a spindle waits to seize the horse and sword. Singers encourage the rider to escape by intensifying the drumbeats and chants.

Al-Tanboora

Al-Tanboora is an African dance brought to Kuwait by Omanis, based on slow pentatonic rhythms. In old Kuwait, a group called “Al-Tanboora” performed in Al-Midan near Darwaza Abdulrazzaq. The dance features a performer wearing a belt of goat hooves tied together, shaking his waist to produce rhythmic sounds in harmony with the music, while leaning on a long stick.

Al-Tanboora Instruments (6 in total):

Tanboora:

A string instrument.

Manjoor:

Made from goat hooves fixed on cloth, tied around the waist of a performer, producing a rattling sound that complements the Tanboora. Four drums accompany the Tanboora player, two on each side.

Al-Ardha Dance

Al-Ardha is a traditional group performance popular across the Arabian Peninsula. It is a collective chant accompanied by movement, performed by large groups of men. Known also as “Al-Hidwa,” it is linked to war chants, encouraging unity and strength. Al-Ardha is performed during national and festive occasions, with men carrying rifles and swords as symbols of pride and defense of the homeland.

Groups in Al-Ardha:

Drum bearers play rhythmic beats in harmony with the chants. In the center stands the flag bearer. The dance involves raising swords, swaying right and left, and stepping forward. Singers stand in rows, accompanied by different drums: large ones called “Takmeer” and smaller ones called “Tathleeth.” Dancers wear belts (Mahzama) once used to carry bullets. Al-Ardha is considered a war dance, with men facing each other in two rows, holding swords or rifles.

Examples of Kuwaiti Al-Ardha Poems:

O homeland, we owe you loyalty, in hardship we seek no intercession.

We keep our lineage strong, even after famine.

If not for God’s will, we would not march, nor drink bitter water.

Our sheikh, we are warriors, igniting battles when needed.

Do not heed false words, rise when called.

Teach discipline, do not falter, stand firm among the people.

And so on...
