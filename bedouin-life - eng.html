Bedouin Life and the Tent (Bayt Al Shaar)

The Bedouin tent is one of the most important shelters created by Bedouins using traditional techniques. It resists desert conditions, is lightweight for transport, and easy to maintain. Women can repair it without help. The covering is made of "Sadu" weaving, with small openings for air in summer. When wet, fibers expand to block water. Smoke escapes through roof openings. The tent is divided by partitions called "Qati". It is rectangular, open on one side (front) and closed on the other (back). Preferred material is goat hair; if unavailable, sheep wool or camel hair is used. Women weave the tent using "Shaqayiq" panels.

Main Components:

Wool and animal hair, with tools like:

Matraq:

Thin stick from Sidra branches, used to clean hair.

Kirdash:

Two wooden pieces with teeth, used to clean hair.

Taghazala:

Wooden piece split at one end, helps spinning.

Mighzal (Spindle):

Smooth wooden stick with metal tip, used to spin hair.

Dajja:

Bundles of spun hair threads, essential for weaving.

Sadu:

Weaving process requiring enough "Dajja" bundles.

Mikhyat (Needle):

Large iron needle about 8 cm long.

Khilal and Marira:

Iron tools similar to needles but larger.

Atnab (Ropes):

Ropes made from wool, used to secure the tent.

Munasib and Fahr (Pegs):

Iron stakes used to fix ropes in the ground.

Mahala (Pulley):

Small wheel of iron or wood, used to pull ropes.

Columns:

Made of tamarisk or acacia wood, 2–2.5 meters long, used to raise the tent.

Bedouin Tent (Bayt Al Shaar)

The Bedouin tent is one of the most important shelters created by Bedouins using traditional techniques. It is resistant to harsh desert conditions, lightweight for easy transport, and simple to maintain. Bedouin women can repair these tents without assistance. The covering is made of "Sadu" weaving, which has small openings that allow air to pass in summer. When wet, the fibers expand, preventing water leakage. Smoke from fire inside escapes through roof openings. The tent is divided into sections by partitions called "Qati". It is rectangular, open on one side (the front) and closed on the opposite side (the back), which is only opened when necessary, such as changing wind direction or seeking shade. The preferred material is goat hair; if unavailable, sheep wool or camel hair is used. Sheep wool and camel hair absorb rainwater, making the tent heavier and harder to rebuild if damaged. Women in the Bedouin community, along with their daughters and companions, weave the tent. It is made of "Shaqayiq" (panels), also called "Shkayij" or "Flejan".

Sadu (Spinning – Dyeing – Drying – Weaving)

Names among Bedouins by regions and tribes:

(( Al-Sameel – Al-Sa’an – Al-Siqa – Al-Qirba – Al-Okka ))

Leathers used for its making:

Sheep skins are generally used, preferably the smaller ones, though larger ones can also be suitable.

Method of making Al-Sa’an:

The sheep or animal skin is carefully flayed, cleaned of meat remains, then filled with a mixture of water + salt + dates (called "Marees"). The skin is sealed, tied, and buried in sand for about three days. Afterward, the wool falls off easily. The skin is emptied, dried, and tanned using a mixture of Arta, Tamarisk bark, and some roots of Salam. Each tribe has its own method. The mixture is boiled, cooled, and the skin is soaked for one or two days until it absorbs the tanning, then dried.

Products made from it:

Al-Sameel or Al-Sa’an for milk or water. Al-Qirba or Al-Badra for water, stitched with sinews. Al-Siqa for milk and butter extraction. Al-Okka for ghee.

Wool and hair weaving:

The process passes through stages: Shearing sheep wool in spring, collecting camel hair in summer. Spinning throughout the year using spindle or taghazala. Dyeing the spun wool, traditionally with desert plants. Weaving (Sadu) usually in summer when Bedouins are more settled, using stretched threads tied to four pegs in a rectangle.

Uses of the fabric:

Bedouins use wool, hair, and goat hair weaving for: – Tents (Bayt Al Shaar). – Qati (partitions inside tents). – Riwaq (windbreak side of the tent). – Adool (food storage bags). – Mazaweed (smaller bags for clothes). – Sanaif (decorative cords for horses and camels). – Zawali (thick wool carpets). – Basat (woven mats). – Aqal (ropes to tie camels). Sadu is one of the oldest traditional crafts, essential for furnishings, cushions, covers, and tents in old Bedouin life.

Coffee and Hospitality

Hospitality is a core part of Bedouin life, a symbol of generosity and social tradition. Guests are welcomed warmly, honored with slaughtered livestock, and served coffee. A famous saying: "The guest upon arrival is a prince, during stay a captive, and upon departure a minister." Guests are protected by the host and his tribe unless honor is violated. The concept of "Milha" (food) means once a guest eats, he is under the host’s protection until he moves to another host.
