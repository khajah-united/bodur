Popular Proverbs

Proverbs condense many stories, values, and wisdom such as patience, generosity, poverty, luck, cunning, and resourcefulness. They are expressed in simple colloquial language to be close to the audience, arising from everyday experiences, struggles, and repeated situations. Proverbs are intangible heritage passed down from generation to generation. They are simple, easy to understand, and reflect the customs and traditions of society, aiming to convey meaning in the fewest words, as the saying goes: "The best speech is brief and meaningful." Popular proverbs often feature humor, exaggeration, or satire to attract attention. They were used in education as tools to instill values, morals, and advice, defining what was acceptable or rejected in society. They transmit the experiences of ancestors and offer simple solutions to problems. Proverbs also play a role in strengthening collective identity, pride in heritage, and preserving words tied to local dialects, documenting history in a narrative easy to memorize. Here you will find a collection of Kuwaiti proverbs, many of which also exist in Gulf cultures, showing how easily they spread and were preserved. My references include Abdullah Al-Nouri’s 1981 collection and explanation of Kuwaiti proverbs, and Saad Ibn Mishlah Al-Azmi’s 2017 "Constitution of Kuwaiti Popular Proverbs."

Sowing in Salty Soil is Wasted

"Subakh" are salty lands where nothing grows and water does not stay. The proverb is used for someone who does good deeds for those who do not appreciate or acknowledge them — meaning the ungrateful.

The Rotten Fish Spoils the Rest

Pronounced with a Persian "J" for the letter K. "Khaisah" means rotten, foul-smelling. The meaning is that one rotten fish will spoil the others. It is used to describe how a person with bad character can corrupt those around them.

The Sun Cannot Be Covered with a Sieve

The sieve is a perforated dish used to sift powdered materials, especially flour. It consists of a circular wooden frame with fine metal mesh. The proverb means that good deeds done by a person will be recognized, and over time the truth about people will be revealed. False masks do not last long.

A Loose Tooth Must Be Pulled

The tooth refers to a molar. "Lamen" means "if," and "Shala’ta" means to extract. The meaning is that when a tooth becomes loose, it must be removed because its pain is bothersome. The proverb is used for two people who were once close but then drifted apart, or for leaving behind someone who is useless and constantly annoying.

Clouds Hover, and God is Merciful

This proverb expresses hope for blessings after hardship, rain after despair, or the fulfillment of a long-awaited wish.

The Heart is a Shop, Everyone Has a Place

It means the heart cannot love all people equally; each has their own place. It is used when someone has many loved ones, such as children or friends, but prefers some over others.

Kohl in an Infected Eye is a Loss

"Ramda" means an eye infected with conjunctivitis. The proverb means that beautifying the ugly only makes it worse, or doing good for the ungrateful is wasted.

Accusations Come While You Sleep

It means accusations may come to you while you are unaware and innocent. It is used for someone who is wrongly accused.

A Date is Not Harmed by Vermin

People believed dates could not be poisoned. "Lahoos" refers to small creatures like spiders or lizards. The proverb is used for someone resistant to illness or danger, or a man accustomed to risks who always emerges unharmed.

The Hen Dies with Her Eyes on the Rice Husks

In Kuwaiti dialect, "diyaya" means hen. "Sabous" are rice husks with grains. The meaning is that when a hen finds husks, she pecks at them searching for grains. If she dies, her eyes remain fixed on them. It is used for someone who enjoyed something and, even after losing it, continues to long for it.

A Garment Longer Than You Will Trip You

It means that if a garment is too long, the wearer risks falling. The proverb is used for someone who associates with those of higher status or power, or trades with those stronger than him, risking harm or loss.

The Donkey Carries Clover but Never Tastes It

"Clover" (Jat) is fodder. The proverb means the donkey that carries clover does not eat it. It is used for someone who produces or prepares something skillfully but does not benefit from it, like a cook who does not eat his own food, or a person whose work benefits others but not himself.

Sand Snake

It is said of someone who pretends to be humble and innocent but is actually deceitful and malicious.

We Heated the Water but the Rooster Flew Away

The meaning: we prepared everything to cook the rooster, but it flew away. Used for someone who prepares for something but loses the very thing he prepared for.

Pull Your Finger, Everyone Will Prescribe Medicine

It means: ask the experts. Used to encourage consultation and seeking advice from those with experience.

The Torment of Hoopoes

Hoopoes are said to still be searching for Solomon’s ring, never resting with food or drink. The proverb is used for someone constantly occupied with endless, repetitive work.

Birds in a Sidra Tree

The Sidra tree bears jujube fruit. The proverb means voices mixed like birds in a tree. Used for children playing noisily or women talking all at once so nothing is understood.

Cheap Fish, Eaten and Blamed

"Awmah" is a cheap fish eaten by the poor. Though tasty, it is avoided by the wealthy due to its abundance and low value. The proverb is used for someone who does all tasks but is still criticized.

Frequent Hammering Breaks the Weld

It means constant hammering breaks what is joined. The weld is the bond between two pieces of iron. Used for gossipers who repeatedly sow discord between friends or spouses until they separate.

Every Time I Drive a Stake, Bad Luck Brings a Stone

The meaning: whenever I try to drive a stake into the ground, a stone blocks it. Used to describe misfortune and lack of success.

Shared Food is Blessed

It means that blessing lies in food shared with others. Used to reflect reality.

Everyone Pulls the Fire to His Own Bread

"Qurais" is a small loaf. The proverb means each person pulls the fire to bake his own bread. Used for selfish people who care only for their own interests.

Dogs Wearing Clothes

Used for groups who appear kind but are actually evil.

Sticky Like Anzaroot

"Anzaroot" is a known resin plant. The proverb is used for someone who clings, becomes tiresome, and never leaves.

After Incense, No Sitting

"Uqob" means after, "Al-Oud" is incense. Among Arabs, incense was the final honor a host offered guests, signaling the end of the visit.

You Have Only Your Nose, Even if Crooked

"Khishm" means nose, "Awaj" means crooked. The meaning: one must accept his defining trait, even if flawed. Used for someone stuck with a reputation or characteristic he cannot escape.

The Drum is Not Beaten Upside Down

"Yitigg" means strike, "Tar" is the drum. Meaning: the drum cannot be played upside down. Used for a frank person who speaks clearly and does not distort the truth.

Colder than Ice

It means extremely cold. Used for a person indifferent, lazy, and unconcerned with what happens around him.

The Wet Man Does Not Fear Rain

It means the matter is already settled; nothing more can affect him.

The Crooked Palm Gives Fruit to Others

"Awja" means leaning. Used for someone who gives benefits to outsiders while his own relatives are in need.

Face to Face, All is Clear

It means: after our meeting, no blame remains; each is free of responsibility. Used when two sides argue or reproach but reach no resolution.

The Beauty of a Garment is in Its Patch

It means a patch should match the garment in color and material; otherwise, it looks ugly. Used for mismatched marriages or unions that bring dissatisfaction.

Camel Dung Smoke

"Jalla" are camel droppings, once used for fire. The proverb warns against being deceived by appearances.

He Gave Him Bitter Drink

"Saqa" means gave to drink. Used for someone who suffers severe harm from a close relative like a spouse, child, or sibling.

Fallen Wall

"Toofa" means wall, "Habita" means fallen. Used for a despised person considered worthless.

Say B, Say Plague in Your Eye

"Uba" means plague. The meaning: when someone opens his mouth to speak, he is cursed and silenced. Used for a strong debater or loud voice who leaves no room for reply.

Crying Does Not Bring Back the Absent

Used for someone who cries excessively over the dead or the absent.

If Everyone Cuts, No Trees Remain

Said of people working outside their specialties, leading to ruin.

Like the Mother of Cats

"Qatawa" means cats. Used for someone restless, always moving from house to house or job to job.

Facing an Army, Not Facing Food

Used for a hungry person sitting before food, waiting for all guests to arrive.

The Dog Returns to the Butcher

The butcher is the meat seller. Used for someone needy who keeps returning to a benefactor yet mistreats him.

Summer Rain

Summer rain is useless and quickly vanishes. Also called "Summer Cloud." Used for someone who comes and goes quickly, or for blessings that reach some but not all.

Afflicted, and the Parasite Seeks Him

"Maqrud" means infested with ticks. The proverb means someone already afflicted continues to attract more misfortune. Used for the humiliated who suffer repeated troubles.

Broken but Endures the Apricot Seeds

"Anqeesh" are apricot pits. Meaning: a tough man who does not flinch even when struck hard. Used for an experienced, resilient person.

Broken Yet Cools Others

Used for someone who suffers hardship or injury yet still helps and advises others.

Long Absence Brings Spoils

It means someone who stays away long returns with abundance and blessings. Used for a person who comes back with good fortune for his family.

Bitten by a Snake, Fears the Rope

"Dabb" means male snake. The proverb means someone bitten once fears even harmless things. Used for someone traumatized who trusts no one.

Never Used Incense, Burned His Clothes

It means someone inexperienced who, when finally trying luxury, suffers misfortune. Used for one whose happiness turns into harm.

Sit by the Blacksmith, Endure the Sparks

"Qaad" means sat. The proverb encourages patience until you achieve your goal.

Poor Planning Mixes Wheat with Barley

Used for a foolish person who cannot manage properly.

A Big Bite Chokes

Used for someone attempting tasks beyond his ability, buying more than he can afford, or taking debts he cannot repay.

Some Work, Others Count

"Takkad" means work. Used for envy, when people resent others’ success and monitor their gains.

This is His Sword, These are His Belts

Used for someone whose situation has not changed despite the passage of time.

One Threatens You, the Other Hits Your Ribs

"Tirou’ak" means frighten you. Used as a metaphor for threats.

Cardamom Without Measure

"Kayl" means weight. Used for someone who gives or takes excessively without considering consequences or the future.

O Fatty, Don’t Spill

"Ya Dahina" means fatty, "Don’t spill" means don’t pour. Used for someone pampered or spoiled.

Walking the Slippery Path, Don’t Trust Not Falling

Used for someone who takes dangerous or deceitful paths without care.

He Eats and Wipes His Hand on the Wall

Used for someone empty-handed who relies on others for sustenance.

Afraid of His Own Shadow

Used for a coward.

From the Liar, You Miss Much Truth

Used for someone known for lying; even when he tells the truth, people doubt him.
